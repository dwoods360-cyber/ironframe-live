> **Executive Summary:** Item 1.05 is a materiality-triggered disclosure control, not an automatic public notice for every intrusion. Recent 2026 filings show registrants determining materiality based on volume and sensitivity of data—even when their own systems were not disrupted—and treating third-party environments as disclosure events. GRC leaders should align Form 8-K processes with Item 106 governance narratives and retain evidence of how materiality was decided.

## I. Exposure Vector

A disclosure committee learns of a ransomware event at outside counsel. Company systems are untouched. Customer Social Security numbers sit in the firm’s matter files. Operations continue. The instinct—“no outage, therefore not Item 1.05”—is exactly the failure mode recent filings challenge.

On 29 June 2026, Navient Corporation reported under Item 1.05 that it became aware on 8 June 2026 of a ransomware incident at a third-party law firm; borrower data including names, dates of birth, addresses, and Social Security numbers were accessed in the firm’s environment; Navient reported no unauthorized access to its own systems and no operational disruption; it nonetheless determined the incident material on 29 June 2026 “in light of the volume and sensitivity of the information involved,” while stating it did not believe the incident had a material impact on financial condition or results of operations. [1]

Other 2026 Item 1.05 narratives similarly emphasise sensitive data environments and potential legal/regulatory consequences even where operational impact is limited or still being assessed. [2]

Separately, Corp Fin’s May 2024 statement clarified that Item 1.05 is intended for incidents determined material; voluntary disclosure of non-material or not-yet-determined incidents should generally use another Item (e.g., 8.01). Trackers through May 2026 show substantial use of Item 8.01 alongside a smaller Item 1.05 population. [3]

## II. Quantitative Context

| Pattern observed in public materials | Governance implication |
| ------------------------------------ | ---------------------- |
| Materiality without registrant outage | Data sensitivity and volume can drive Item 1.05 even when ops continue [1] |
| Third-party hosted / professional-firm incidents | Vendor and counsel incidents can be registrant disclosure events [1][2] |
| Materiality date ≠ awareness date | Record both; the four-business-day clock runs from materiality determination [4] |
| Item 8.01 then later Item 1.05 | Voluntary early communication ≠ final materiality conclusion [3] |
| Statement of no material financial impact alongside Item 1.05 | Materiality is broader than immediate P&L hit; still requires careful committee analysis [1][3] |

| Process clock | Rule |
| ------------- | ---- |
| Discovery / awareness | Starts investigation and escalation—not automatically the 8-K clock |
| Materiality determination | Starts the Item 1.05 four-business-day period; must be without unreasonable delay after discovery [4] |
| Filing | Item 1.05 content: nature, scope, timing, material impact or reasonably likely impact |
| Amendments | Update when required information later becomes available |

## III. What Modern GRC Must Enforce

### Suggested newsletter sections (control translation)

| Theme | What filings teach | Control requirement |
| ----- | ------------------ | ------------------- |
| Materiality without shutdown | Ops continuity ≠ immaterial [1] | Materiality rubric includes data sensitivity, individuals affected, legal/regulatory exposure, reputational harm |
| Data sensitivity vs direct financial loss | Committees cite sensitivity even when financial impact is not yet quantified [1][2] | Separate “material incident” from “material financial statement impact” analyses |
| Third-party incidents | Law firm / hosted app compromises can be registrant events [1] | Intake paths for vendor and counsel notices; tabletop a third-party scenario |
| Unauthorized AI use involving sensitive data | Emerging fact pattern in 2026 commentary—verify case-by-case in EDGAR before asserting any specific issuer | Include shadow-IT / unsanctioned AI in incident taxonomy |
| Item 7.01 / 8.01 vs 1.05 | Voluntary paths exist; mis-tagging creates confusion [3] | Playbook: when to use 8.01 vs wait for 1.05 |
| Materiality before investigation ends | Filings acknowledge incomplete facts with amendment commitments [2] | Decision memo template for interim materiality |
| Item 106 alignment | Annual governance text must match real committee practice [4] | Quarterly board/cyber committee cadence with minutes that show challenge |
| Disclosure-committee evidence | Process quality is itself an enforcement focus | Retain timeline, attendees, materials reviewed, determination rationale, counsel input |

### Do not overclaim

* Not every cyber incident belongs under Item 1.05.
* Do not collapse discovery, materiality determination, the four-business-day filing period, and amendments into one date.
* Do not treat this newsletter as EU AI Act guidance; unsanctioned AI appears here only as a potential incident vector inside US securities disclosure.

Human-in-the-loop attestation before executive publication remains the institutional control: materiality is a governed decision, not a scanner alert.

## IV. Verification Protocol

1. Re-read Navient’s Item 1.05 narrative on EDGAR for awareness date, materiality date, third-party scope, and financial-impact language. [1]
2. Confirm Form 8-K Item 1.05 instructions: materiality determination without unreasonable delay; four business days thereafter. [4]
3. Confirm Corp Fin’s distinction between Item 1.05 and voluntary Item 8.01 disclosure. [3]
4. For any additional issuer examples, cite EDGAR accession numbers—not secondary blogs alone.
5. Keep DORA, CSRD, CPS 230, and AI Act applicability out of this newsletter’s lead subject.

## Key Takeaways

* Recent filings show materiality driven by sensitive data and third-party environments—not only outages.
* The disclosure clock starts at materiality determination; record the decision path.
* Item 106 governance claims should be reconcilable with how the disclosure committee actually works under pressure.
