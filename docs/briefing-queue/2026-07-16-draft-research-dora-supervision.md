> **Executive Summary:** The Digital Operational Resilience Act is past the “write the policy” phase. In its second year of application, competent authorities are positioned to test whether ICT risk frameworks, incident processes, testing programmes, and third-party controls produce usable evidence. Entities should prepare for supervisory review across common evidence domains while recognising that ECB/SSM, banking, insurance, securities, and national authorities will not run identical examination scripts.

## I. Exposure Vector

An in-scope financial entity completed a 2024–2025 DORA implementation programme. Policies exist. The Register of Information was submitted. Contracts were partially uplifted. Tabletop exercises were scheduled once.

In 2026, the exposure shifts from design completeness to supervisory credibility:

* Can management demonstrate that the ICT risk-management framework is used in decisions—not only approved once?
* Are major ICT-related incidents classified and reported on required timelines with retained decision logs?
* Is resilience testing risk-based, documented, and linked to remediation?
* Where threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) applies or is scheduled, is there a credible multi-year plan?
* Are ICT third-party arrangements complete as to contractual clauses, register quality, concentration, and substitution?
* When supervisors ask for evidence, can the entity produce operating artifacts—tickets, test reports, board minutes, remediation trackers—or only policy PDFs?

DORA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554) entered into application on 17 January 2025 for in-scope financial entities’ digital operational resilience obligations, including ICT risk management, incident handling and reporting, resilience testing, and ICT third-party risk. [1][2]

Critical ICT third-party provider oversight at EU level has also moved into active designation and supervisory architecture; ESAs’ designation activity in late 2025 is a signal of operational oversight maturity, but it does **not** mean every financial entity faces one uniform “EU DORA exam” on a single calendar. [3]

**Title discipline:** This briefing discusses supervision and examination readiness. It does not assert a formal, EU-wide programme branded “year-two NCA exams.”

## II. Quantitative Context

| Signal | Interpretive use |
| ------ | ---------------- |
| Application since 17 January 2025 | Second calendar year of live obligations by July 2026 |
| Register of Information and contractual remediation programmes | Common supervisory entry points for ICT third-party risk |
| TLPT planning toward designated-entity completion expectations later in the decade | Multi-year testing investment, not a single desktop exercise |
| Parallel NCA/SSM methods | Same regulation; different evidence requests and escalation paths |

Do not invent a single EU penalty figure as “the cost of DORA failure.” Exposure is entity-specific: supervisory measures, remediation mandates, concentration risk in critical ICT providers, and operational disruption costs. Boards should quantify open remediation items by severity and dependency on critical functions—not by aspirational percentage-complete dashboards alone.

## III. What Modern GRC Must Enforce

### Evidence domains supervisors are likely to test

| Domain | Examination-ready artifacts |
| ------ | --------------------------- |
| ICT risk-management framework | Approved framework, risk assessments, control inventories, management reporting cadence |
| Board and management accountability | Minutes showing challenge, risk appetite linkage, resource decisions, escalation of material ICT risk |
| Incident classification and reporting | Classification methodology, clocks, notification packs, post-incident reviews |
| Resilience testing | Annual testing plan, results, deficiencies, retest evidence |
| TLPT (where applicable) | Designation status, scheduling plan, scoped scenarios, remediation from findings |
| ICT third-party registers | Complete, reconcilable register aligned to reporting requirements |
| Contract completeness | Clause gap analysis vs DORA contractual expectations; uplift tracker |
| Concentration and substitution risk | Dependency maps, substitutability assessments, exit options |
| Remediation tracking | Aged findings with owners, due dates, residual risk acceptance |
| Operating effectiveness | Samples showing policies used in incidents, changes, procurements, and board packs |

### Label supervisory differences

| Authority context | Practical implication |
| ----------------- | --------------------- |
| ECB / SSM (significant institutions) | Often deeper ICT and outsourcing scrutiny integrated with banking supervision |
| National competent authorities | Local priorities, templates, and enforcement ladders may differ |
| Insurance / securities / other sectoral supervisors | Same DORA baseline; different portfolio risk focus and information requests |
| Critical ICT third-party oversight (EU level) | Distinct from entity-level exams; still affects concentration and contractual leverage |

### Control-system requirements

| Failure mode | Requirement |
| ------------ | ----------- |
| “DORA project closed” in 2025 | Continuous evidence production and remediation ownership |
| Register quality treated as a one-off filing | Reconcile register to contracts and critical functions ongoing |
| Testing without remediation closure | Link findings to tracked corrective actions |
| Identical playbook assumed for all NCAs | Maintain a core evidence library; adapt packs to the asking authority |

Continuous DORA readiness visibility for board reporting is a practical response path: entities that can show operating evidence—not only policy approval—are better positioned when supervisory letters arrive.

## IV. Verification Protocol

1. Confirm DORA application date of 17 January 2025 from authoritative EU/ESA materials. [1][2]
2. Do not claim a single harmonised “year-two NCA examination cycle” across the Union unless a specific authority’s published programme is cited.
3. Separate entity supervision from EU-level critical ICT third-party oversight. [3]
4. Keep CPS 230 (Australia), the AI Act, CSRD, and SEC cyber disclosure out of this briefing’s lead claims.
5. For any country-specific assertion, cite that NCA’s guidance or public supervisory communication.

## Key Takeaways

* Mid-2026 DORA work is supervisory evidence work.
* Prepare common evidence domains; expect different asking styles across authorities.
* Registers, contracts, testing, incidents, and remediation closure are where policies meet examination.
